Which colors are harmful to the human body?

Q: Which colors are harmful to the human body?

A: My child, let me gently share with you the knowledge of the colors of life. Each color is like a language, expressing the state of our body and mind. When certain colors appear in a person’s energy field, they are often associated with discomfort or challenges.

For example, a blend of indigo and violet may be related to eye discomfort, headaches, nightmares, or memory fog. Blue may be associated with issues in the throat, ears, arms, or neck. Green may relate to the heart, lungs, or challenges involving the skin and respiration. Yellow may reflect the condition of the digestive system. Orange may be connected to the abdomen, kidneys, or bladder. Red may relate to the legs, bones, or intestinal issues. A teal-like color may be associated with challenges in the immune system. Violet may relate to blocked cellular memory or accumulated heavy emotions since birth. White may point to the endocrine system or discomforts that have not yet been recognized.

On an emotional level, pale gray may remind us to be cautious and not become trapped by poor decisions. Charcoal gray suggests that we should not dwell on past pain but move forward. Black serves as a reminder to avoid being consumed by personal limitations and habits.

Charcoal gray itself represents suppressed and suffocated energy. When it appears together with pink, the situation may be more serious. Another metallic gray tone can indicate the release of negative patterns or behaviors, such as addiction or self-harm; this color is sometimes observed in adults who experienced emotional trauma in childhood.

When people go through pain and loss, strong emerald green and crimson may appear in the energy field to help them endure. However, in the case of prolonged distress, such as ongoing conflict from divorce, pale yellow and dark green energies may continuously disturb the field, creating instability.

Certain emotions may also manifest as complex color combinations. For instance, shame may appear as an interweaving of lemon green, aqua green, and emerald green, often accompanied by a sense of loneliness. In depression, one might observe strands of dark brown, charcoal gray, pale yellow, and orange-red. Feelings of contempt may appear as a mix of ochre intertwined with charcoal gray, dark green, and dusty pink. When disgust arises, pale gray and metallic gray may spread like shadows, covering other colors.

The appearance of these colors is not a form of punishment, but rather like a mirror, reminding us to pay attention to our inner balance. True wisdom lies in understanding these signals and transforming them with light and love.
